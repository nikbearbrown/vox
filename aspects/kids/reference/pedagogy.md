# Pedagogy — the research behind each Gate K rule

Distilled from the developmental literature; each rule cites its lineage so a
future editor knows what breaks if the rule is relaxed.

## Video deficit (why the recurring host + camera address)
Children under ~2.5 learn substantially less from video than from an identical
live demonstration (Anderson & Pempek 2005; Barr 2010). The gap narrows with:
a familiar consistent on-screen character addressing the viewer directly
("you"); repetition ACROSS sessions (parasocial relationship ≈ live partner);
reduced perceptual clutter. → Laws 1, 2, 8. One mascot per series, forever.

## Pacing (why 4–9s beats and one stimulus)
Lillard & Peterson (2011, Pediatrics): 9 minutes of fast-paced cartoon
measurably impaired 4-year-olds' executive function immediately after —
mechanism is edit pace and stimulus-novelty rate, not content. → Law 2:
4–9s teaching beats, ≤1 new stimulus/beat, static background, no rapid cuts.
Commercial kids' pacing optimizes retention; this pipeline optimizes
acquisition. Do not compromise toward "energy."

## Contingency (why question → pause → confirm)
The Blue's Clues / Dora research program (Anderson et al., UMass CRITC):
a character who asks and WAITS produces better engagement and recall than
broadcast narration — para-interactivity activates social-learning channels
without real input. → Law 3: 2–4s of genuine silence; the confirm line works
for answerers and non-answerers alike.

## Category learning (why varied exemplars + one label + a contrast)
Early-categorization work (L. B. Smith and colleagues; Gershkoff-Stowe &
Smith on word-object mapping): children generalize from VARIED exemplars
under a CONSTANT label; one canonical image repeated teaches that image, not
the category. Contrast cases sharpen boundaries faster than positives alone.
→ Law 4: 3–5 exemplars varying irrelevant dimensions, verbatim label word,
exactly one "this is NOT ..." case near the end.

## Music (why one short recurring jingle)
Melody chunks information (alphabet-song effect) — but continuous underscore
is the overstimulation problem again. Music as structural scaffold = a short
(<15s) melodic phrase tied 1:1 to the concept, reused verbatim so it becomes
a retrieval cue. → Law 5. Silence under labeling/pause beats is correct.

## Screen-time norms (why the co-viewing beat and the caps)
AAP "Media and Young Minds" (2016, periodically revised): limit solo exposure
before ~2; co-viewing and interaction encouraged at all ages. → Laws 6, 7:
one explicit do-it-with-a-grown-up beat per episode; 1–3 band ≤4 min,
4–5 band ≤8 min. Check the current AAP statement before major format changes.

## Mayer (general multimedia hygiene)
Coherence (no decorative extras), signaling, redundancy principles generalize
down: everything on screen serves the concept. → reinforces Laws 2 and 4.


## Concept x age map (developmental alignment — Gate K enforces)
| band | appropriate | too abstract / risk |
|---|---|---|
| 1-2 | basic colors (red, blue, yellow); simple matching | shapes and numbers; rote counting has no cardinality yet |
| 2-3 | big-three shapes (circle, square, triangle); basic sorting | rectangles/ovals over-generalize; numerals |
| 3-4 | rectangles + ovals; cardinality 1-3; shape attributes | formal handwriting; multi-digit anything |
| 4-5 | cardinality 1-10; multi-attribute sorting; secondary colors | excessive sedentary runtime |

Risk flags (hard):
- NO shape-drawing tutorials for 1-3: a child can RECOGNIZE a circle at 2 but
  cannot draw one until ~3 (square ~4, triangle ~5). Receptive recognition and
  matching only — never show the viewer a motor task they cannot perform.
- NO numerals before ~2; for the young band teach "three things in a set,"
  never symbolic numeral recognition or counting routines.

## Positioning (AAP / WHO, sharpened)
AAP: avoid non-video-chat screen media before 18 months; 18-24 months only
high-quality media WITH an adult. WHO: no sedentary screen time at 1, <=1h at
2. Therefore the "1-3" band is really CO-VIEWED 24-36 months — say so in the
series description; it is not a stand-alone format for 12-17 months.

## Production grammar (from the live-teacher model)
- Locked camera; no pans/zooms during teaching.
- GAZE is the attention cue: host addresses the camera on hello/question/
  coview beats, and looks AT the exemplar on teach beats (camera_address
  false on teach is correct design, not a violation).
- Sentence-final double label: "The ball is red. Red." — label last, then
  repeated alone, same cadence every exemplar.
- Anchor beat before exemplars (the pure swatch/card); consolidation beat
  after (spoken recap of the exemplar list, NO montage); return-to-target
  card at the close.
- The question-pause beat uses a NEW exemplar (retrieval WITH transfer), and
  during a choice question the host's hands stay down — pointing leaks the
  answer. The reveal comes after the pause, slowly.
- Contrast case is a MINIMAL PAIR (blue ball mirroring the red ball — one
  changed dimension), gets no highlight ring and no point.
- One highlight indicator max per exemplar (brief ring, ~1.5s, then gone).
- Host never wears/carries the target color outside the exemplars (the
  black-and-white penguin is the perfect color-episode host by construction).
- Jingle ~70 BPM; pauses hold ROOM TONE, not digital dead silence.
